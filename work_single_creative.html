Maudie

Home / Works / Maudie

Description

Maudie was a joy to design. Before we knew that Sally Hawkins and Ethan Hawke were to play Maud and Everett I had already begun to envision the wardrobe for the two idiosyncratic characters.

Inspiration came from rural Nova Scotia landscapes, the ocean, the fog, the little fishing boats, the colourful homes, the hooked rugs and knitting the women of the east coast embellished their lives with.

Maud’s colour palette was inspired by her paintings. Her tones were bright, lively, filled with joy. Maud would have ‘kept up’ her clothes; little print aprons piped with bright solid trim, a perky beret for going to town, a pretty bag reminiscent of her middle class life before Everett, smart shoes with a little buckle, a heel; as much as her crippled feet could manage.

Everett was a man of the earth, cutting wood, hauling a fish cart, and splitting wood. Everett would have been too cheap to buy new. His clothes would have been hand-me-downs from the church basement. His layers of long john, rough plaid shirts and rugged overcoats would be roughly mended or worn with holes; another layer added to keep out the chill.

My research began in the Art Gallery of Nova Scotia in the Maud Lewis Gallery, then I turned to Archival photographs and the CBC Documentary with the real Maud & Ev. On-line I found a wealth of research of fishermen & east coast life.

I sourced all the fabrics to give a period authenticity using vintage fabrics from my personal collection. I rented authentic period clothing and even borrowed BILLY’S Oilskins from a local fisherman.

Texture and colour, wear and tear were important with all the costumes for leads and for background in order to create a full picture of the times. Costumes were hand painted, dyed and broken down to compliment the landscape we filmed in. We built Maud a prosthetic inside her costume to mimic her posture as it degenerated throughout her lifetime.

I think my two favourite elements were Maud’s aprons that we made in every decade to cover the few outfits she wore. The intention was to keep her look simple, a reflection of her modest lifestyle. The little aprons were her whimsy. The other was Everett’s red coat. Every man in Maud’s paintings is Ev, her hero, chopping wood, driving a team, tapping maple trees. And in every painting Ev is in his red coat.

Lastly my little ‘a ha!” find was a brooch for Maud, a painters palette with an array of brushes. I saw that very brooch on the real Maud in the CBC documentary. And there it was on-line!
